What is Hypothesis Testing?

Hypothesis testing is a systematic statistical procedure used to determine whether the results obtained from a sample provide sufficient evidence to support or reject a claim about a population.


The process begins by stating two competing statements: the null hypothesis (H0), which assumes no effect or no difference, and the alternative hypothesis (H1), which represents the research claim. Using sample data, a test statistic is computed and then compared with a predetermined critical value, which acts as a threshold for deciding whether the observed result is statistically extreme under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. If the test statistic exceeds this critical value, the null hypothesis is rejected in favor of the alternative.


The decision can also be supported by examining the p-value, which indicates how likely the observed result would be if the null hypothesis were true. A smaller p-value provides stronger evidence against the null hypothesis.

Z-Test in Hypothesis Testing

A z-test is a statistical procedure used to evaluate whether the sample data provides enough evidence to reject a chosen null hypothesis (H₀) in favor of an alternative hypothesis (H₁). The hypotheses are defined by selecting the comparison symbol (≥, ≤, =) and the test direction (one-tailed or two-tailed).


The z-statistic is computed using the sample mean (x̅), sample size (n), and the known population standard deviation (σ). These values determine how far the sample mean is from the hypothesized condition under H₀, measured in standard error units.


The calculated z-value is then compared with the critical value based on the selected significance level (α). This allows you to identify whether the result falls within the rejection region.
